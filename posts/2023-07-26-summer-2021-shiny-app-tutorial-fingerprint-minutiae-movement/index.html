Summer 2021 Shiny App Tutorial: Fingerprint Minutiae Movement by Examiner and Print

A tutorial on how to use the interactive data visualization app I created during my Summer 2021 research experience, which depicts fingerprint minutiae movement by examiner and print

Sherry Huang
2023-07-26

Introduction

In this blog, I’m going to walk through how to use and understand the Shiny app I created during my Summer 2021 research experience. If you’re unfamiliar with Shiny apps, they are web applications created using R where you can show your data analyses and visualizations. And if you’re unfamiliar with the research I did in Summer 2021, you can read my blog about it here.

Finding My Shiny App

There are a couple different ways to access my Shiny app: via this hyperlink, typing in asluby.shinyapps.io/Minutiae-Movement into your search bar, or typing in bit.ly/fingerprintimputation into your search bar. Regardless, they should all take you to the same place.

Scatterplot of Fingerprint Minutiae Markings

I am not able to replicate my entire Shiny app on my website, but I will try my best to replicate the visualizations found on the app. When you first open the app, you will see the following scatterplot:

This scatterplot displays all the markings forensic fingerprint examiners make when they examined a latent and exemplar print to determine if they are matches, or from the same individual, in this White Box study conducted by the FBI. Due to privacy reasons, we aren’t allowed to view the fingerprints the examiners looked at, but if we imagine a Cartesian plane on top of the fingerprints and markings, this is exactly what the scatterplot is, just without the fingerprints. The points represent the markings the examiners made in the Comparison and Analysis stages. The hexagons behind the points are a way to show the density of markings in an area. A darker hexagon represents an area with a lot of markings across the examiners, and a lighter hexagon represents an area with few markings. We can also think of a darker hexagon as an area where many examiners agree that there is a unique and notable feature, thus resulting in many markings in that area; similarly, a lighter hexagon is an area where there may not be many unique and notable features, and so few examiners mark that area. The lines represent any “movements” of markings between stages of examination. This is useful for my research in imputation bias, as this could provide evidence that examiners could be changing markings to support their own conclusions, especially when they are given an exemplar print to examine alongside the latent print in the Comparison stage. The color represents minutiae markings marked in the Analysis stage in this specific scatterplot. There are different characteristics of a fingerprint that examiners look for and mark, such as minutiae, delta, core, etc. You can change which part to highlight by selecting a feature on the left of the Shiny app under “Feature Type.” Note that this is not possible in the scatterplot shown here on my blog; this only shows the minutiae feature type. Also note that in some prints, there may be none of a certain feature type. And finally, you can also hover your mouse over a marking to see which examiner marked it and at which stage.

The title of the plot includes the study’s label for the prints examined: M010003. The “M” in the label lets us know that the latent and exemplar prints are indeed matches in this case, and the numbers are the unique labels for the latent and exemplar prints.

Thus, this first scatterplot shows the markings made by several examiners (and how they are moved) at two different stages of examination on a latent and exemplar print that are matches. If you want to look at different prints, simply select a different pair from the drop-down menu at the left of the Shiny app under “Pair ID.” Note that this is not possible here on my blog; this only shows the pair “M010003.” Also note that if you pick a pair that begins with “N,” the latent and exemplar prints are not matches in that case.

Scatterplot of Minutiae Markings by Examiner

Above the scatterplot on the Shiny app ( not on this blog), there are three tabs. Currently, the “Print” tab is selected. This is the scatterplot shown in the previous section, and it shows the minutiae marked by all the examiners who looked at this latent and exemplar print. However, we may be interested in how each examiner marked the prints. We can do this by selecting the “Examiner” tab above the scatterplot.

The default scatterplot shown above displays all the same information as the first scatterplot, but instead of looking at markings by all examiners who looked at pair M010003, this scatterplot only displays markings by one examiner, specifically examiner EA1338, on the pair M01003. Here, the hexes and line segments offer more information on how each examiner marks fingerprints. For example, notice that there is only one dark hexagon. This demonstrates that the examiner marked minutiae in areas where few other examiners marked minutiae. While this doesn’t necessarily mean that the examiners minutiae markings can’t be trusted, this could be a pattern we look for in the examiner’s other fingerprint markings. Also notice that there are only 2 line segments. This indicates that the examiner rarely moved any of their markings between stages. Again, while this doesn’t mean that the examiner is not susceptible to imputation bias, this is another pattern we can keep in mind when looking at the examiners’ other print markings. In general, these scatterplots are a good way to get an idea of examiners’ patterns when marking prints. You can change which examiner’s scatterplot you want to see by selecting a different examiner from the drop-down menu under “Examiner ID” at the left of the Shiny app.

The examiners will change when you change the pair ID because different examiners viewed different fingerprints in this study. Currently, there is no great way of looking at one examiner’s scatterplot across different prints. When designing this app, I chose to have it centered around being easier to compare markings across different prints, and not across one examiner.

Summary of Print Conclusions

The last tab above the scatterplot on the Shiny app ( not on this blog) is the “Summary” tab. This gives a table depicting the conclusions of the examiners’ findings in this study. Below is the summary table for the pair ID M010003.

% Correct % No Value % Inconclusive % Change in Value % Very Easy % Easy % Moderate % Difficult % Very Difficult # Examiners
1 100 0 0 0 13.33 46.67 33.33 6.67 0 15

The first column is simply the number of rows, which should just be \(1\) for every summary table. The second column is the percent of examiners who got the conclusion correct (i.e., they correctly identified the prints as matches or non-matches). In this specific summary table, \(100\%\) of the examiners correctly identified this pair as matches. The third column is the percent of examiners who identified the print as “No Value,” which means that they felt that the latent print “is not of value for individualization and contains no usable friction ridge information.” In this specific summary table, \(0\%\) of the examiners identified the latent print as “No Value.” The fourth column is the percent of examiners who were inconclusive about their determinations. “Inconclusive” means that “[t]he comparison [determined] that neither individualization nor exclusion is possible.” In this specific summary table, \(0\%\) of the examiners were inconclusive about their results. The fifth column is the percent of examiners who changed their conclusions between the analysis and comparison stages. For example, an examiner might identify a latent print as “VEO,” which means that “the impression is of value for exclusion only and contains some friction ridge information that may be appropriate for exclusion if an appropriate exemplar is available.” However, after the comparison stage, the examiner might change their conclusion to “VID,” which means “that the impression is of value and is appropriate for potential individualization if an appropriate exemplar is available.” I’ve included this in the summary table because this could be potential evidence of imputation bias – especially with a switch from NV (No Value) or VEO (Value for Exclusion Only) to VID (Value for Identification) because NV/VEO means that the latent print is not appropriate for individualization. In this specific summary table, we can see that \(0\%\) of the examiners had a change in value across stages. The next five columns (columns six through ten) are the percentage of examiners who picked each comparison difficulty. In this specific summary table, \(13.33\%\) examiners found the comparison very easy, \(46.67\%\) easy, \(33.33\%\) moderate, \(6.67\%\) difficult, and \(0\%\) very difficult. And finally, the last column shows the number of examiners total in the study that looked at that specific latent and exemplar print. In this specific summary table, \(15\) examiners looked at this specific pair M010003.

I’ve included this summary table because it gives a good birds-eye view of how examiners performed on this pair of prints. For pair M010003, the general conclusion appears to be that it was an “easier” comparison, and every examiner got it right. With other pairs, we could potentially use the table to find evidence of imputation bias, such as the “\(\%\) Change in Value” column or “\(\%\) Correct” column. To view the summary table of other pairs, simply select a different pair from the drop-down menu under “Pair ID” at the left of the Shiny app.

Conclusion

This Shiny app was designed as part of my Summer 2021 research looking at imputation bias in the forensic fingerprint examination process. The scatterplots and tables show movement and changes in minutiae markings between stages, which is evidence of imputation bias. In general, however, my goal was to create an easy, visual way to represent and understand the idea of “imputation bias.” If you have any further questions, please feel free to reach out to me.
